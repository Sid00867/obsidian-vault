The paper's main contribution is **adaptive recursive subgoal refinement** for long-horizon offline goal-conditioned RL. Instead of using a fixed hierarchy (e.g., always three subgoals), it repeatedly asks whether the current goal is reachable according to a learned **reachability estimator**. If not, it inserts an intermediate subgoal and recursively repeats the process until the next target is considered easy enough for the low-level goal-conditioned policy to execute. The key insight is that the number of subgoals should depend on task difficulty rather than being fixed. Reachability is defined in terms of the learned policy's ability to reach a target; not geometric distance, making the planning adaptive to the controller's capabilities.

A major limitation is that the planner **does not synthesize novel subgoals**. Candidate subgoals are constrained to states already present in the offline replay buffer, with a learned module selecting promising intermediate states and the reachability estimator deciding whether to execute or refine further. This makes the approach conservative and stable, since it avoids planning through out-of-distribution states, but it also fundamentally limits expressiveness: if the dataset lacks suitable intermediate states, the planner cannot invent new ones. As a result, the method resembles **learned retrieval over a library of observed states with adaptive recursion**, rather than a planner that reasons about or generates abstract intermediate objectives.
